Who Will Win the 2026 AFL Brownlow Medal?

The 2026 Brownlow Medal market has been turned on its head at the halfway point of the season. Bailey Smith has surged from $7 outsider to $4.00 outright favourite — one of the most dramatic midseason market moves in recent memory. Here is everything you need to know: the odds, what has changed, and where the value is.

Our Verdict · 24 May 2026

Bailey Smith ($4.00) is the new favourite and the form justifies it. But our value is Marcus Bontempelli at $4.50 — equal price with Daicos, but the better claim. The Dogs are building toward top four, Bontempelli is producing elite midfield output, and the market has undervalued his four-quarter consistency. At any price under $5, Bontempelli is a bet.

The Big Market Move: Bailey Smith Overtakes Daicos

For the first 10 rounds of the 2026 AFL season, Nick Daicos was the assumed favourite. He had been runner-up in consecutive Brownlow Medal counts — 2024, 2025 — and the widely held view was that 2026 would finally be the year the medal matched the reputation. He was priced accordingly: $3.50 heading into Round 11, the shortest Brownlow price the market had given him.

Then the market moved. And moved hard.

By Round 12, Bailey Smith had become the $4.00 outright favourite — having started the season at $7.00. Daicos had drifted to $4.50. Bontempelli, who had been $6.00, had shortened to $4.50. Three players are now genuinely in contention, but Smith leads for the first time, and the question is whether the market is right to back him so heavily.

Why Smith? The Case for the New Favourite

Bailey Smith's surge to the top of the Brownlow market is built on substance, not just narrative. Since arriving at Geelong, he has been one of the competition's most consistently polled midfielders — picking up votes in consecutive rounds in a way that the Brownlow market responds to decisively.

The qualities that make Smith a Brownlow contender are well understood. He is an elite contested ball winner — not just statistically but in terms of the type of contested ball that umpires reward. He wins it at the coalface in traffic, distributes quickly and accurately, and then resets to do it again and again across four quarters. He doesn't disappear in close games. He doesn't vanish under pressure. These are the marks that umpires give 3-vote performances for.

The Geelong connection matters too. At his former club, Smith was competing for touches in a midfield that had other Brownlow-calibre players. At Geelong, he is the engine. When the Cats win, he is typically their best player — and their 2026 form has been strong enough to deliver big wins against quality opposition. Those are exactly the games where votes accumulate.

The key risk is straightforward: suspension or injury would end his Brownlow campaign. His aggressive contested style creates suspension exposure. But as the market stands, it is priced for him to remain available — and the odds reflect genuine, sustained form, not a single standout performance.

The Case for Bontempelli (Our Value Pick)

Marcus Bontempelli at $4.50 is our recommended bet in this market. He is equal price with Nick Daicos but offers a materially better argument for why he wins.

Bontempelli is the complete Brownlow package. He is a four-quarter midfielder in a winning team, producing the kind of high-volume, clean-disposal, contested game that umpires have always rewarded. He has been close to the Brownlow multiple times. In 2026, the Dogs are building strongly — their midfield depth is among the best in the competition and Bontempelli is at the heart of it.

The statistical argument is compelling. When Western Bulldogs win, Bontempelli is almost invariably their best player. He plays every game with ferocious intensity and rarely has an off night. From Round 15 onwards, when the Brownlow market typically hardens, the Dogs' run of games against finals-bound opposition will give Bontempelli multiple opportunities to bank three-vote performances.

At $4.50 — the same price as Daicos — Bontempelli is the better value. He doesn't carry the Daicos baggage of two consecutive runner-up finishes. He is not relying on his team to recover form. And he is, by the midpoint of 2026, producing output that demands umpire attention in every game he plays.

What Has Gone Wrong for Daicos?

Nick Daicos drifting from $3.50 to $4.50 tells a story that punters need to understand before dismissing him outright. The drift is not about his individual form — Daicos remains an elite midfielder by every measure. It is about context.

Brownlow votes are not just awarded for quality. They are awarded for quality in the context of matches that draw umpire attention and, increasingly under the 2026 rule change, matches where the statistics support a dominant performance. Collingwood's mixed 2026 season has reduced the number of games where Daicos can dominate a compelling, high-stakes contest and walk away with 3 votes. When the Pies lose tight games, the votes go elsewhere.

The pattern of two consecutive runner-up finishes also weighs on the market psychologically — perhaps unfairly, but the price reflects it. Daicos at $4.50 is not a bad bet. If Collingwood find form in the second half of the season and he continues performing, he will poll strongly. But the drift from $3.50 is justified, and the gap between him and Smith/Bontempelli represents a genuine reassessment of who is best placed to accumulate votes across the remaining rounds.

The Rest of the Field: Where Is the Next Value?

Below the top three, the market has a long tail. Here is our assessment of the key players:

Christian Petracca — $10.00

Petracca has gone from being a Brownlow certainty at Melbourne to a $10 speculative runner at Gold Coast, and the price reflects genuine uncertainty. His individual talent is beyond question — he is one of the best midfielders in the competition when he is right. But his Brownlow case now depends entirely on whether the SUNS can deliver him big-game wins against quality opposition in the second half of the season. If they can — and there are signs they might — Petracca at $10 is excellent value. If not, he is a good player on a team that doesn't give him enough high-vote games.

Zak Butters — $13.00

Butters has drifted slightly from $10 to $13 as Port Adelaide's fortunes have dimmed, and the drift is logical. Fewer important wins means fewer high-vote opportunities. But Butters individually continues to perform at an elite level. His disposal efficiency and contested game are Brownlow-quality. If Port strings together a late-season run — not impossible — Butters at $13 becomes genuinely interesting.

Max Holmes — $21.00

Holmes at $21 is an intriguing speculative play. His midfield game at Geelong has developed rapidly and he is polling in a team that is winning. The $21 represents significant overlay against his recent form trajectory, though he lacks the proven polling history of the top five contenders.

Jai Newcombe — $34.00

Hawthorn's breakout midfielder has been one of the stories of the 2026 season. At $34, he represents genuine outsider value if Hawthorn continue to perform as a top-four side. A run of 25+ disposal, multiple clearance games in a winning team is exactly what can push a $34 shot into contention by Round 20.

Lachie Neale — $34.00

Neale is a proven Brownlow winner — he took the medal in 2020 and polled well in multiple seasons after. At $34, he is being dismissed too readily. The Lions are a finals team and Neale remains their engine. A late-season run of form could see him move quickly.

Key Brownlow Rules: What You Need to Know

Before betting on the Brownlow, these rules directly affect the market:

  • Suspension disqualifies. Any player who receives a suspension during the home-and-away season is ineligible to win. This is a material risk for contested midfielders — including Smith — who play an aggressive style.
  • Statistics access in 2026. For the first time, field umpires can access approved match statistics before casting their votes. This benefits players with elite output metrics — possessions, clearances, disposals — and is expected to make the voting more aligned with the statistical best performer in each game.
  • Home team bias historically matters. Umpires have historically been shown to vote slightly more for home team players. Teams with multiple home games in the second half of the season may benefit their Brownlow contenders.
  • Finals form does not count. Only home-and-away season games contribute votes. Finals performances, no matter how dominant, do not affect the Brownlow count.

When to Bet the Brownlow

Brownlow betting timing matters significantly. The market tightens as the season progresses and popular contenders accumulate form, so the best value is typically available either at the start of the season (when prices are longer and uncertainty is highest) or mid-season when a non-favourite has a clear run of form but hasn't yet been fully priced in by the market.

The current midseason market presents a specific opportunity: Bailey Smith is now the favourite at $4.00, but has he been fully priced in? The case for Bontempelli at equal money ($4.50) is that the market has responded to Smith's form but hasn't fully reappraised Bontempelli's, who has been quietly shortened from $6 while Smith attracted all the headlines.

By Round 18-20, the favourite-at-the-time principle will dominate — the player leading in votes will drift in as the market hardens. If Bontempelli is polling well, $4.50 now will look very short by then.
