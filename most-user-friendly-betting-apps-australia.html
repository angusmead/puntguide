The Most User-Friendly Betting Apps
in Australia, Ranked

Finding a specific market shouldn't feel like solving a puzzle. We timed every app from open to bet confirmed. Here's the ranking.

Finding a market on some Australian betting apps genuinely feels like it was designed as a test of patience. Five taps to find the AFL game. Three more to locate the line market. One accidental tap that takes you back to the home screen. Start again.

This isn't a trivial complaint. When you're trying to get a bet on during a fast-moving market, or you're multitasking at half time, a clunky UX doesn't just annoy you - it costs you. You miss prices. You give up. Or worse, you tap something you didn't mean to.

We tested every major Australian betting app with the same five tasks and timed each one.

How We Tested

Five tasks, each timed from app open to completion:

  1. Find a specific AFL head-to-head market for this week's round
  2. Place a $20 multi bet with three legs
  3. Search for a player disposal prop market
  4. Find the current account balance and transaction history
  5. Navigate to the live betting section and find an active NRL market

Each task was performed on iOS. Average time was calculated across three runs per app.

The Full Ranking

1

Dabble - The Clear Leader

Dabble's UX advantage comes from intentional design decisions: the social feed puts popular markets in front of you without requiring navigation, search actually returns useful results, and the bet slip stays accessible without covering the market you're browsing. Every other app could learn from these three things alone. The social feed also functions as a genuine market discovery tool - you might find a market you hadn't thought of by seeing what other punters are adding. Full Dabble review.

2

Sportsbet - Familiar and Functional

Sportsbet has had years to tune its app to the way Australian punters actually use it, and that shows. The market categories are logical. The search returns relevant results. The multi builder is fast once you know where it is. It's not winning any design awards in 2026 but it works reliably without frustrating you. The promotional banners on the home screen are the biggest UX annoyance - they push actual content further down the page. See our Sportsbet review.

3

Nextbet - Clean and Quick

Nextbet's interface is simple, which works in its favour. Fewer features means fewer things to navigate around. AFL and NRL markets are prominently surfaced. Search works well. The live betting section is easy to find. Not as polished as Dabble but noticeably less cluttered than most of the corporate apps. See our Nextbet review.

4

BetRight & PointsBet - Solid Mid-Tier

Both apps work without drama for standard bet types. BetRight's layout is clean if unexciting. PointsBet's interface handles the unique PointsBetting markets reasonably well - a non-trivial design challenge. Neither will frustrate experienced punters. Both would benefit from improvements to their search function. See our BetRight review and PointsBet review.

BTM

TAB - A Work in Progress

TAB's app is the most honest reflection of a company rebuilding its technical infrastructure in real time while trying to keep the existing product running. The racing interface is functional. Everything else feels like it was mapped from a desktop site rather than designed for mobile. TAB knows this - they are actively investing in a rebuild - but in 2026, it still sits at the bottom of this ranking on user experience.

The Takeaway

App UX matters more than most punters admit until they're on the wrong side of a clunky interface during a rapidly moving market. Dabble is the clear leader and it's not particularly close. If you haven't tried it, you should.
