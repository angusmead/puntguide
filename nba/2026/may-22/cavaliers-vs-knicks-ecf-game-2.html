What Happened in Game 1

The Cavaliers led by 22 points and somehow lost in overtime. It was a jaw-dropping collapse, and not just because of the scoreline - the manner of it was brutal. New York found a single tactic that Cleveland had no answer for, and ran it 16 times in one quarter.

The Knicks targeted James Harden with pick-and-roll actions, using Mikal Bridges or OG Anunoby to set screens and forcing Cleveland into repeated Harden-on-Brunson switches. Harden was picked off 27 times across the game - more than in any game of his 17-year career. He allowed 1.53 points per possession when switched onto Brunson. When Brunson had him in space, that number rose to 1.80.

Donovan Mitchell scored 29 with six steals in a career-high. But Cleveland's coaching staff allowed the Knicks to run the same action eight straight times without an effective counter. Atkinson called a timeout late and briefly tried doubling Brunson, which opened kick-out threes for Bridges and Anunoby. There was no clean answer found.

The Harden Problem - Can Cleveland Fix It?

The Harden screen issue isn't unsolvable - but it requires the Cavaliers to change their defensive principles, and Kenny Atkinson has not shown an ability to make significant mid-series adjustments in these playoffs. It took Cleveland 15 games just to get here.

The clearest solutions on film are: fight through the screens rather than concede the switch, and force Bridges and Anunoby to actually set firm contact or stay home. Harden and Wade need to work over the top instead of surrendering position immediately. These aren't complicated - they're just disciplined execution under pressure, which is exactly what collapsed in Game 1.

The coaching matchup is the decisive factor. Mike Brown found the answer on the fly in Game 1 while trailing by 20. Atkinson has the film now but has shown a pattern of stubbornness - Sam Merrill (who was -17 in Game 1) will almost certainly play significant minutes again. That's a gift to New York.

New York's Platform to Win the Series

The Knicks had a bad offensive game in Game 1 - and still won. They made 32-of-56 three-point attempts (57.1%) but the real damage came in the paint (60 points) and at the line (55 combined free throws). When Brunson is hunting Harden, those numbers spike further.

New York's bench was excellent. Mike Brown deployed Landry Shamet at the right moments, made the defensive call that broke Cleveland open, and managed the late game perfectly. The Knicks are now on a 7-game playoff winning streak. Home court. Momentum. A clear tactical edge. Everything points their way in Game 2.

Team News - Both Rosters Full Strength

No injuries listed for either team ahead of Game 2. Donovan Mitchell - who walked gingerly to the locker room after Game 1's overtime thriller - has been confirmed to play. The ankle concern has cleared and Cleveland will have their best player available. Knicks likewise have a clean bill of health with no absences.

A fully fit Mitchell (29 points, 6 steals in Game 1) makes the Cavs more dangerous offensively but doesn't solve the Harden problem. The structural coaching disadvantage and Brunson's blueprint remain the deciding factors regardless of Mitchell's availability.
