Key Factors

1. The Injury Landscape

Before you look at anything else, the injury situation shapes everything. De'Aaron Fox has not played a single minute in this series with a high ankle sprain. Dylan Harper — who had 24 points and 7 steals in Game 1 before going down — is questionable with a hamstring injury. That forces Stephon Castle to carry the entire offensive creation burden against the best defensive team in the NBA.

For OKC, Jalen Williams played just 3 minutes in Game 2 before leaving with hamstring tightness. He is their second-best player and without him the Thunder's offense runs almost entirely through SGA — who averaged 7-of-23 from the field in Game 1 before adjusting in Game 2.

2. Wembanyama at Home

In Game 1 on the road he put up 41 points and 24 rebounds in a double-overtime war. In Game 2, gassed from OT and playing away again, he still managed 21 points, 17 rebounds, 6 assists and 4 blocks. At Frost Bank Center with a rested crowd and his own familiar floor, Wembanyama is a different animal. His home postseason record of 7-0 with a 99.7 defensive rating around him makes San Antonio the right side at -1.5.

3. Castle's Turnover Crisis

Stephon Castle logged 11 turnovers in Game 1 and 9 in Game 2 — 20 total, the most by any player in the first two games of a Conference Finals in the modern era. OKC's defensive scheme specifically targets primary ball-handlers and forces decisions under pressure. Without Fox as a secondary creator and with Harper uncertain, Castle faces that pressure alone in Game 3. The turnovers suppress scoring possessions and skew the Under.

4. The Under Case

Both regulation games in this series came in under 216.5 (Game 1 regulation was 202 points). The Spurs' postseason defensive rating of 99.7 is the best among all remaining teams. Without Williams, OKC runs fewer plays through their second offensive option. Castle's turnovers kill possessions. This is the kind of series made for the Under.
