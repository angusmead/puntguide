Key Factors

1. The Bench Gap Is Not Sustainable for San Antonio

Oklahoma City's reserves have outscored San Antonio's bench 183 to 64 across three games. That's not a single-game aberration — it's a structural mismatch. In Game 3 alone the OKC bench put up 76 points, the most by any bench in a Conference Finals since 1971. The Spurs are leaning on Wembanyama for 41+ minutes per game because they simply don't have the depth to compete with the Thunder's rotation. That kind of usage catches up with anyone, even Wemby.

2. Fox and Harper Carrying Injuries Into a Pressure Game

De'Aaron Fox and Dylan Harper are both less than 100% heading into Game 4. Both played in Game 3 but were visibly limited, particularly in the second half when the game opened up. When Wembanyama is the only healthy creator and the Spurs need to come back from a 2-1 series deficit, their offence has nowhere to hide if Wemby is in foul trouble or gassed. OKC's defence will target exactly that vulnerability.

3. OKC on the Road — The Pattern Is Clear

Oklahoma City is 5-0 on the road in the 2026 playoffs and 6-0 as underdogs across the entire season. They have shown repeatedly that they don't need home crowd noise to execute their system. Shai Gilgeous-Alexander handles hostile environments better than any player at his age in recent memory. The $2.19 price treats this as a coin flip on San Antonio's floor — the historical pattern says otherwise.

4. Wembanyama Fatigue Is Real

Playing 41+ minutes per game at 22 years old in the Conference Finals against the deepest bench in the NBA is not sustainable. He is the Spurs' offence, defence, playmaker and shot-blocker simultaneously. By Game 4 that physical toll will show — and OKC's fresh legs up and down their rotation will wear San Antonio down over four quarters.
